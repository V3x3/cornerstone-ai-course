Somewhere around 2024, something changed in consulting proposals.

Every major firm adopted AI for research, slide production, and proposal drafting. The tools were good. The output improved across the board. Partners who had previously relied on junior consultants to produce polished first drafts now had access to polished first drafts in hours.

The intended outcome was differentiation: whichever firm adopted AI fastest would produce better work than competitors still relying on manual processes.

The actual outcome was convergence. Within eighteen months, proposal quality across major strategy firms had converged upward. Clients could no longer distinguish firms on analytical rigour, formatting quality, or the comprehensiveness of their benchmarks. Everything was polished. Everything was well-structured. Everything cited the same McKinsey and BCG reports that AI had been trained on.

The firms that win new mandates now are the ones where a partner says something unexpected in the pitch meeting. Something that makes the client think: *how did they see that?* Not because AI produced it — but because a human with genuine judgment, who had absorbed information AI processed and connected it to context AI doesn't have, produced an insight that felt genuinely original.

The equilibrium shifted. Most consultants are still optimising for a game that ended.

## The logic of Nash Equilibrium

John Nash proved that in many competitive situations, there exists at least one stable state where no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy alone, assuming all other players hold theirs constant.

This sounds technical. The practical content is uncomfortable: equilibria can be terrible for everyone.

The gas station price war is the classic example. Station A lowers prices to attract customers from Station B. Station B responds in kind. Station A lowers again. The war continues until both are operating at margins that don't cover their costs. Neither station can unilaterally raise prices without losing customers. Neither wants to continue. The equilibrium is stable and mutually destructive.

The consulting proposal arms race follows the same logic. Firm A invests in better research, longer appendices, more detailed benchmarks. Firm B matches it. Firm A increases slide quality. Firm B adds more graphs. Nobody wins more clients — because the clients' reference point adjusts to whatever the new standard is. The equilibrium is expensive, exhausting, and produces no differentiation.

Both equilibria are rational at the individual level and damaging at the system level. Every firm that adopted AI for proposal quality was rational. The collective effect was equilibrium without advantage.

## When the equilibrium resets

Nash Equilibrium is not static. It resets when something changes the underlying payoff structure.

AI is changing the payoff structure of most knowledge work. When any skill becomes widely accessible — when everyone can produce the same quality output using the same tools — the equilibrium around that skill breaks and reforms around whatever scarce resource has now replaced it.

This has happened before. In the 1990s, the ability to build Excel models was a differentiator for finance professionals. By 2005, everyone could build Excel models, and the differentiator had shifted to the judgment about which model assumptions mattered. The equilibrium reset.

The same reset is happening across knowledge work with AI. The skills that were scarce — clean writing, structured analysis, comprehensive research — are becoming abundant. The equilibrium is reforming around whatever is not AI-replicable: original pattern recognition, political judgment, genuine client relationships, the ability to say something uncomfortable that turns out to be right.

The professionals who gain in this reset are not the ones who used AI earliest. They are the ones who correctly identified where the new equilibrium would form and began building that skill before the reset completed.

## Identifying your Nash trap

The question is not whether you are in a Nash trap — most knowledge workers are, in at least some dimension of their work. The question is whether you can see it.

Nash traps have a characteristic texture: you are competing intensely for something that used to differentiate but no longer does. The effort is high. The returns are diminishing. But stopping unilaterally feels like ceding ground.

Signs you are in a Nash trap:
- You spend significant effort on a deliverable quality that clients or colleagues have stopped specifically noticing.
- You feel that competitors are always matching your upgrades, and your upgrades are always matching theirs.
- The standard for "adequate" keeps rising but the reward for exceeding it stays constant.

In consulting, the trap is often presentation quality. In policy, it is often document length and citation density. In law, it is often the thickness of due diligence files. In finance, it is often model complexity. In each case, the output was once a signal of quality. When everyone produces the same quality, it stops being a signal and becomes table stakes.

## The escape from the trap

Nash traps are escaped by finding value in a space where the competitive dynamics have not yet compressed it.

The escape is not to produce the same kind of output more efficiently. That is the equilibrium trap at higher speed. The escape is to ask what the *new* game rewards — what skill, judgment, or relationship is becoming scarce as AI makes existing skills abundant — and to build that before the rest of the market catches up.

This requires temporarily underoptimising for the current equilibrium. The consultant who stops spending hours polishing a deck that clients have stopped distinguishing from AI-generated decks, and instead invests that time in developing the political judgment or relationship depth that constitutes the new differentiator, looks less impressive in the short term and is building the more durable position.

It also requires correctly identifying the new equilibrium. This is harder than it sounds. The most obvious adjacent skills are usually already crowded. The most valuable moves are the non-obvious ones.

If you removed your name from your work and showed it alongside a competitor's AI-generated output, could your manager tell the difference?

If not, you are in a Nash trap. The question is what you are going to do about it.
