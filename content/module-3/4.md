A managing director at a Frankfurt consulting firm reads a report in early 2025 predicting that AI will eliminate thirty percent of entry-level consulting work within five years. His analysis is quick and feels shrewd.

First-order: reduce junior hiring. The work can be done by AI. Why pay for analysts when the AI is faster, cheaper, and available around the clock? His cost structure improves. Clients notice. He looks ahead of the curve.

He presents the plan to the executive committee. They approve it.

He has not considered what happens next.

## The second-order blind spot

If he had extended his analysis one round further, he would have noticed something uncomfortable: every major consulting firm in his competitive set has read the same report and is thinking through the same first-order response. Reduce junior hiring. AI does the work. Costs improve.

But junior consultants are not only workers. They are also the raw material from which future senior consultants are made. The engagement manager who became a partner in 2023 was a junior analyst in 2015, doing exactly the work the managing director now wants to eliminate. Remove the juniors, and you have removed the training ground for the people who will be running client relationships in 2033.

If every firm follows the first-order logic — and they will, because it is obvious and immediately rewarding — the pipeline of experienced consultants dries up across the industry by the early 2030s. The individuals who did get junior training become extraordinarily scarce. Their salaries spike. Firms compete fiercely for a talent pool that nobody adequately replenished.

The managing director who cut junior hiring in 2025 will spend 2032 paying enormous premiums for experienced consultants his industry failed to develop.

He will not blame his 2025 decision. He will blame the talent market.

## Why second-order thinking is rare

Howard Marks, the investor, describes second-order thinking as "the most reliable source of investing edge." The first-order thought — the obvious implication of an event or trend — is available to everyone who pays attention. It gets priced in. The advantage is in the thinking that most people stop before reaching.

This is a general observation about how professional advantage is created. Most people think one step ahead. Some think two. Very few think three.

The scarcity of second-order thinking is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of habit and incentive structure. First-order thinking produces conclusions faster. It satisfies the pressure to decide, to act, to appear decisive. The managing director who says "AI will replace juniors, therefore reduce hiring" sounds like a leader responding to technological change. The one who says "we should think carefully about what happens to our pipeline in 2030" sounds hesitant.

Organisations reward first-order decisiveness far more reliably than they reward the slower, less visible work of thinking further.

## Three orders applied

The example of junior consulting hiring illustrates how to apply the framework explicitly.

**First order:** AI eliminates entry-level analytical work. Reduce hiring. Costs fall. Competitive position improves in the short term.

**Second order:** Every firm reduces hiring simultaneously. Pipeline of future partners depletes. Experienced consultants become scarce.

**Third order:** Firms compete fiercely for scarce experienced consultants. Salaries rise dramatically. The handful of firms that maintained junior hiring — accepting short-term cost disadvantage — now have the experienced talent pipeline that competitors lack. The juniors who got trained become disproportionately valuable.

The person who saw the third order in 2025 and optimised for it wins the largest in 2032. They accepted cost disadvantage and looked behind the curve. The payoff was years away and invisible for most of the period.

This is the characteristic of second- and third-order advantage: it requires tolerating an intermediate period where the wrong bet looks right. The managing director who maintains junior hiring while competitors cut will look inefficient for four years. Then he will look prescient.

## AI as a second-order thinking tool

There is a practical application here that most professionals are not using.

AI is an excellent partner for extending analysis beyond the first order. The constraint on second-order thinking is not intelligence — it is the effort required to hold multiple time horizons and causal chains in mind simultaneously. AI can scaffold this process.

The prompt structure: "I'm considering [decision]. What are the first-order consequences? For each, what are the second-order consequences? For each of those, what are the third-order consequences? For each consequence, what would need to be true for it to materialise?"

Apply this before any significant professional decision — a hiring choice, a strategic pivot, a capability investment, a response to a competitive development. The output will not always reveal something you missed. Sometimes the first-order analysis genuinely is adequate. But the discipline of asking ensures you don't miss the cases where it isn't.

The managing director should have spent thirty minutes running this analysis before presenting his plan. He would have found the pipeline problem. He might still have chosen to reduce hiring — perhaps the short-term financial pressure was real enough that he had no choice. But he would have known what he was trading and planned for it.

## The AI talent market in 2032

The second-order dynamics of AI adoption in professional services are not hypothetical. They are playing out across industries simultaneously, with first-order decisions being made by thousands of leaders who have not run the second-order analysis.

The entry-level work being eliminated today is the training ground for the judgment that will be scarce in a decade. Every firm cutting juniors because AI can do their tasks is also cutting the apprenticeship model that developed senior judgment. The senior judgment that AI cannot replicate — client relationship management, political navigation, genuine strategic insight — is acquired through years of doing the work AI is now eliminating.

The professionals who understand this are either already senior, in which case they need to think about what they are building behind them, or still early in their careers, in which case they need to position themselves in the training pipelines that remain intact.

Everyone in your industry is thinking about what AI will do next year.

Almost nobody is thinking about what it will do to the talent market in 2032.

That gap is where advantage lives.
