A senior consultant at a Dutch firm closes a significant deal with a long-standing client. The next day, she sends a handwritten note. Not an email. A note, on paper, in her own handwriting, thanking the client specifically for the trust they placed in the firm and for a particular moment in the final negotiation that she found impressive.

It takes her three minutes.

Six months later, the client mentions it at a board meeting. Not the deal — the note. He says: "You know what I actually find impressive about that firm? They still do things you can't outsource."

Meanwhile, every other firm in the competitive set has sent AI-drafted follow-up emails. Perfectly worded. Perfectly structured. Perfectly forgettable.

The note was a signal. The emails were noise. The difference was not in the content — it was in what each one cost to produce.

## Spence's insight

Michael Spence won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2001, partly for his work on signalling theory. The key insight: in markets with information asymmetry — where one party knows more about the quality of something than the other — signals are credible only when they are costly to fake.

Spence developed this primarily through the labour market. A job applicant has a university degree. The employer cannot directly observe the applicant's ability. But a degree is a credible signal because obtaining it requires four years of time and significant money — a cost that a low-ability applicant would be unwilling to bear. High-ability applicants can bear it because the investment will pay off. The signal works because it is costly, and the cost is correlated with the underlying quality it signals.

Remove the cost, and the signal collapses. If a degree could be obtained in a weekend with minimal effort, it would tell employers nothing. Its credibility is a function of what it takes to produce.

## What AI does to signals

AI collapses signalling costs.

Before AI, a polished piece of written work — a well-structured proposal, a thoughtful follow-up memo, a precisely argued analysis — was a credible signal of quality. Producing it required time, skill, and attention. When you received such a document, you inferred something about the person who produced it.

A beautifully written email now costs nothing to produce. Which means it signals nothing.

This is not primarily about deception — it is about information. The recipient of AI-generated communication cannot infer what the sender would have been willing to invest, because the sender may have invested almost nothing. The signal has been decoupled from the underlying quality it was supposed to indicate.

For consulting firms, this is an existential problem. The McKinsey-quality deck was a credible signal of McKinsey-quality thinking because it was produced by McKinsey-quality analysts working long hours. When AI produces a McKinsey-quality deck, the deck is no longer a signal of the thinking behind it. The deck is noise.

## The new credible signals

If AI collapses the cost of what was previously expensive to produce, the signals that retain credibility are those that AI cannot produce cheaply — or at all.

**Original thinking.** AI synthesises existing knowledge; it does not generate genuinely novel insights about specific situations. The consultant who says something in a client meeting that could only have come from having listened carefully, thought independently, and connected information in a non-obvious way is signalling something real. AI cannot fake that specific signal.

**Genuine human connection.** The handwritten note works because the client knows the consultant could not have automated it. The effort of writing by hand, the specificity of the reference to a particular moment, the deliberate choice of an analogue channel — each element of the signal is costly in a way that AI use is not. The cost is the signal.

**The willingness to say something risky.** AI optimises for acceptability. It produces output that is unlikely to offend, challenge, or discomfort. A human who says something uncomfortable, specific, and true — in a client meeting, a board presentation, a policy briefing — is doing something AI genuinely cannot do. The risk taken is itself the signal.

**Demonstrated judgment over time.** This is the slowest but most durable signal. A track record of being right in ambiguous situations, of having called things that turned out to matter, of having shown good judgment when the outcome was uncertain — this cannot be faked by AI and cannot be acquired quickly. It compounds.

## The professional implication

Most professionals are currently investing heavily in signals that AI is about to devalue. They are producing better-formatted documents, more comprehensive analyses, and more thoroughly researched presentations — the things that were previously costly to produce well.

These investments are not worthless. But their signalling value is declining at an accelerating rate. The incremental advantage from being marginally better at AI-replicable output is decreasing.

The question worth asking — specifically and honestly — is what you are doing that AI cannot replicate efficiently. What, in your professional work, still involves a cost that AI cannot absorb? Where are you still producing genuine signals rather than AI-assisted noise?

This is a harder question than it sounds. Most knowledge workers, examined carefully, are producing a great deal of noise — output that looks like high-quality work but is differentiated only in ways that AI can now match. The signals that still carry information tend to be the ones that involve physical presence, personal risk, or accumulated judgment over time.

The handwritten note worked because it was three minutes the consultant chose to spend in a way that AI couldn't. That choice, and what it signalled about how she thought about client relationships, was worth more than any email.

Everything AI makes easy to produce, AI makes worthless as a signal.

What are you doing that is genuinely hard?
