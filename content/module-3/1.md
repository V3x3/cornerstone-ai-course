Two junior consultants at a Frankfurt strategy firm are staffed on the same engagement. Both are smart. Both know the informal rule: only one person gets promoted this cycle, and it's probably whoever impresses the senior partner most on this project.

So they play accordingly.

Consultant A hoards the client data model she built. Consultant B claims credit in the Monday update for an analysis they ran together. Consultant A drops a subtle comment about Consultant B's methodology in front of the partner. Consultant B does the same to her two days later. Neither of them is villainous. Both are rational actors responding sensibly to the incentives they perceive.

The client notices something is wrong. Meetings have a strange undercurrent. Documents keep overlapping. There are two slightly different versions of the same framework floating around. The engagement is rated "below expectations."

The promotion goes to a third consultant, not on the project at all.

They played the game correctly. They lost because they were playing the wrong game.

## The mechanics of the dilemma

The Prisoner's Dilemma is one of the most studied scenarios in game theory, and for good reason: it captures something true about human cooperation that pure logic misses.

The setup: two players can either cooperate or defect. If both cooperate, both get a decent outcome. If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector wins big and the cooperator loses badly. If both defect, both lose — but moderately.

The perverse logic: from any individual player's perspective, defection is always the rational choice. Whatever the other player does, you're better off defecting. If they cooperate, you win big by defecting. If they defect, you limit your losses by defecting too. Pure logic says: defect.

And yet, when both players follow this logic, they produce an outcome worse for both of them than mutual cooperation would have achieved.

This is not a mathematical curiosity. It is the structural description of how professional competition works in organisations where people mistake individual optimisation for strategic thinking.

## Why this changes with repetition

The single-round Prisoner's Dilemma predicts defection. But professional life is not a single round.

In the 1980s, political scientist Robert Axelrod ran a series of computer tournaments, inviting game theorists to submit strategies for iterated Prisoner's Dilemmas — hundreds of rounds with the same opponent. The winning strategy across multiple tournaments was disarmingly simple: Tit for Tat. Start by cooperating. Then do whatever your opponent did last round.

Nice, but not naive. Cooperative by default, retaliatory when defected against, forgiving enough to return to cooperation when the defection stops.

What Axelrod found matters enormously for professional life: in repeated interactions, cooperation is not just morally preferable. It is strategically superior. The players who cooperated over multiple rounds systematically outperformed those who defected.

The Frankfurt consultants weren't in a single-round tournament. They were starting careers at a firm where they would interact with each other, and with colleagues who watched them, for years. The reputation they built on this engagement was going to follow them. They acted as if the round was ending. It wasn't.

## The AI shift

Here is where game theory meets the current moment in a way most professionals are not thinking about carefully enough.

AI is commoditising individual analytical output at a speed that is restructuring the payoff matrix.

Three years ago, the consultant who produced the clearest strategic framework, the tightest data model, the most persuasive slide deck had a genuine edge. Individual analytical quality was scarce and therefore valuable.

That scarcity is collapsing. AI produces clean slide decks, tight frameworks, and coherent data models for anyone who knows how to prompt it. The consultants in Frankfurt are discovering what every knowledge worker will discover within a few years: when both players can produce the same quality analytical output, the differentiator shifts to something AI cannot replicate.

Trust. Collaboration. The ability to build relationships with clients and colleagues that outlast any single engagement.

The payoff matrix has shifted. In a world where individual output is commoditised, the player who cooperates has access to something the defector doesn't: a reputation for being good to work with, a network of people who will advocate for them, the compounding advantage of relationships built over years.

The defector optimised for a scarce resource that stopped being scarce.

## What cooperation actually requires

This is not an argument for being nice. It is an argument for being strategic about when cooperation produces the better outcome — which, in professional life, is far more often than the competitive instinct suggests.

The people who cooperate well in professional environments share a few characteristics. They are honest about shared work. They give credit publicly when credit is due. When they have a legitimate competitive interest, they pursue it directly rather than through subtle undermining. They distinguish between situations where genuine competition exists (for a specific, fixed resource) and situations where the resource is actually expandable through cooperation.

Most professionals conflate these categories. They treat recognition, relationships, and professional reputation as fixed resources — if someone else gets more, they get less. In reality, the opposite is often true: the person who makes the team look good tends to look better, not worse.

The consultants in Frankfurt were competing for a single promotion slot. That part was real. What wasn't real was the assumption that winning required making each other look bad. They could have cooperated on the engagement, both looked excellent, both built a strong relationship with the client, and let the promotion decision be made on track record rather than internal politics. One of them would still not have been promoted that cycle. But their reputation across the firm would have been completely different — and, in the iterated game of a career, that matters far more than a single round.

## Cooperation is not weakness

There is a persistent confusion in professional life between cooperation and naivety. The Tit for Tat insight corrects this.

Cooperation is the strategic default. But it comes with a clear condition: defect when defected against, cooperate again when cooperation resumes. This is not passivity. It is the combination of goodwill and credibility that makes long-term cooperation stable.

The person who cooperates unconditionally gets exploited. The person who defects by default destroys the relationships that long-term advantage depends on. The person who cooperates by default and retaliates proportionately when needed turns out, in the long run, to win more.

The person you're competing with at work is probably not your real opponent. Your real opponent is the assumption that it's a competition at all.
