## The exercise

This module covered game theory, Nash Equilibrium, signalling, second-order thinking, and the meta-game. Now you apply them to the professional situation you are actually in.

The goal is not to produce a polished strategy document. The goal is to see your professional situation more clearly than you did before. Complete honesty is the only requirement.

---

## Part 1: Name the game (10 minutes)

Describe the competitive game you are currently playing at work. Be specific. The game is not "succeeding in my career" — it is the particular thing you are competing for, with whom, by what rules, in what timeframe.

**Write down:**

- What specific resource or outcome are you competing for? (A promotion, a mandate, a client relationship, a budget, recognition, internal influence — be exact.)
- Who are the other players? (Not generically "my colleagues" — name the specific people or groups whose choices affect your outcomes.)
- What are the informal rules? Not what the employee handbook says, but what actually determines who wins this game in your environment.
- What does "winning" look like in the next 12 months?

---

## Part 2: Map the equilibrium (10 minutes)

Now describe the current equilibrium: the stable state where everyone is optimising according to the current rules.

**Write down:**

- What is everyone in your competitive set doing to win this game? What is the standard approach?
- Is this equilibrium producing good outcomes — for you, for the organisation, for the clients or mission? Or is it a Nash trap where everyone is working hard and nobody is winning?
- What would happen if you unilaterally changed your strategy? Would you gain, or would you just be penalised for not playing by the rules?

---

## Part 3: Find where AI has already shifted the rules (10 minutes)

AI is not a future disruption to most professional environments — it has already changed the payoff matrix in specific ways that most people have not explicitly mapped.

**Write down:**

- Name one task or output in your professional game that AI has already made easier, cheaper, or more accessible. (Be specific — not "analysis in general" but "the specific kind of competitive benchmark we used to spend three days on.")
- Has that change shifted what winning the game requires? Or is everyone still competing on the dimension that has been commoditised?
- Name one thing about your professional value that AI has made *more* scarce — something that was previously taken for granted but is now harder to find because AI has automated the adjacent work.

---

## Part 4: Identify your meta-game question (5–10 minutes)

The meta-game is the question of whether you are playing the right game at all.

**Write down:**

- Is the game you described in Part 1 still the game worth optimising for? Or is there evidence that it is being disrupted, automated, or becoming irrelevant?
- If you look twelve to thirty-six months forward: what will the game reward then, that it does not reward now?
- What is one thing you are currently spending significant time on that will be worth less in that timeframe? What would you spend that time on instead?

---

## Reflection prompt (5 minutes)

Before submitting: read back what you wrote in Part 1. Then read back what you wrote in Part 4.

Is there a gap between the game you are currently playing and the game that is worth winning?

If yes — what is one concrete thing you could change in the next thirty days?

You don't need to answer that question here. You just need to be honest about whether you know what it is.
