A newly promoted engagement manager at a consulting firm in Amsterdam spends her first six months working harder than she ever has.

She is producing excellent analysis. She's the first one in the office and the last to leave. She stays across every detail of every workstream. When junior analysts struggle, she steps in and fixes it herself — because she knows exactly how to do it and it's faster. When clients ask technical questions, she answers them directly and precisely. She is being, in every measurable way, an exceptional analyst.

She is being rated as a disappointing engagement manager.

Her senior partner explains it in a brief conversation that takes her by surprise: "You're solving the wrong problems. An analyst's job is to produce excellent analysis. A manager's job is to develop analysts and build client relationships. You're not doing either."

She has been playing brilliantly. She has also been playing the wrong game — the one she won to get the promotion, not the one the promotion required her to start playing.

## The meta-game is the game about which game you're playing

Most professional development advice focuses on how to get better at your current game. Be more productive. Develop sharper skills. Build stronger relationships. Deliver more consistently.

This advice is useful, but it misses something fundamental: the game changes. Not once, but repeatedly, as careers evolve, industries shift, and technology disrupts established patterns.

The meta-game is the capacity to step outside your current game and ask: is this still the right game? What are the actual rules here? What does winning look like at the level I'm currently operating, rather than the level I was at previously? And — critically in the current moment — which parts of this game are being automated away, and what new game is emerging to replace them?

Most professionals never ask these questions explicitly. They receive a set of rules early in their career — produce excellent work, be reliable, build expertise — and optimise within those rules for years, sometimes decades. The rules change around them, but because they are absorbed in playing well, they don't notice.

## Career stages as different games

The engagement manager's error is structural, not individual. The transition from analyst to manager is a game change that almost everyone navigates poorly on the first attempt.

As an analyst, the game rewards: technical precision, comprehensive analysis, intellectual rigour, efficiency in producing output. The player who produces the most excellent analysis most reliably wins.

As a manager, the game rewards: developing team members, maintaining client confidence, managing multiple simultaneous workstreams, making judgment calls under uncertainty, knowing when to escalate and when to absorb. The player who is the best individual analyst is often not the player who wins this game — because the skills that make someone an excellent analyst can actively interfere with the skills required to develop other analysts and build client trust.

This transition is well-documented. It is also reliably disorienting, because the previous game's success created patterns of behaviour that feel correct — because they were correct, for a different game.

The meta-game question: is the game I'm optimising for the game I'm actually in?

## AI as a meta-game disruptor

AI does not just change the rules of existing games. It changes which games are worth playing.

Some games are being eliminated. The game of "produce the most comprehensive competitive analysis" is being automated. The game of "draft the most polished proposal" is being automated. The game of "research the most thoroughly" is being automated. These were real games, with real winners, generating real professional advantage, for decades.

If you are optimising for a game that AI is automating, you are the engagement manager trying to produce excellent analysis after being promoted to engagement manager. You are playing brilliantly, in a game that no longer rewards what you're doing.

The meta-game question, applied to AI: which parts of my current professional game are being automated, and what new game is emerging at the frontier of what AI cannot yet do? Am I building skills in the second game, or am I still optimising for the first one?

## The meta-game audit

The meta-game audit is a discipline, not a one-time event. Professional games change faster than they used to. The audit should run quarterly.

**The winning conditions question:** What does it actually mean to succeed at my current level, in my current role, in my current organisation? Not what the job description says — but what actually produces recognition, advancement, and influence? Identify the specific behaviours, outputs, and relationships that the organisation genuinely rewards.

**The obsolescence question:** Which of those winning conditions are becoming accessible to AI? Which are being undermined by AI? Which are being made more valuable by AI?

**The game-change question:** Is there a game change coming — a promotion, a restructuring, a technological shift — that will require playing a different game? If so, what does that game reward?

**The positioning question:** Given the answers above, what is the one thing you could start doing in the next ninety days that would most improve your position in the new game?

The Amsterdam engagement manager needed six months and a candid partner conversation to realise she was in the wrong game. The meta-game audit is the alternative: systematic, quarterly, requiring no outside intervention.

## Which game are you in

The consulting firm example is vivid, but the dynamic applies everywhere.

The NGO programme officer who has been rewarded for ten years for deep expertise in vocational training is playing a game that may be changing as AI produces adequate training assessments faster than she can. The EU policy analyst who built her reputation on the quality of her written briefings is in a game that AI is directly entering. The lawyer who was rewarded for the comprehensiveness of his due diligence has less of a moat than he thinks.

None of these are reasons to panic. They are reasons to run the audit.

You are currently optimising for something. The question is whether you know what it is. And whether it is still the thing that wins.
