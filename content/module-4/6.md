## The exercise

This module covered antifragility, the Hydra principle, optionality, Black Swan positioning, and the barbell strategy. The audit you are about to complete is not a theoretical exercise — it is an honest assessment of where you actually stand.

Be as specific as possible. Vague answers ("I'm fairly adaptable") are not useful here. Concrete answers ("sixty percent of my visible output is a specific type of work that AI has already begun to replicate") are what this exercise is designed to produce.

---

## Part 1: The fragility map (10–15 minutes)

Map your current professional position honestly.

**Work through each question:**

**On your core output:**
- What fraction of your visible professional output — the work that your manager, clients, or colleagues see and evaluate — could be produced at adequate quality by AI tools available today?
- Is this fraction growing or shrinking? If you compare today to two years ago, are you more or less replaceable in the dimension of output quality?

**On your expertise:**
- Your expertise is either deepening in a domain, or it is stable. If it is stable, AI is closing the gap between you and a well-prompted AI in that domain over time.
- Is the domain you are expert in growing in importance, stable, or contracting?
- If your primary domain contracted by thirty percent over the next five years, what would you do? Do you have a specific answer, or a vague one?

**On your relationships:**
- The relationships you have built — with colleagues, clients, stakeholders, the people who will advocate for you when opportunities arise — are a genuine antifragile asset. AI cannot replicate your specific relationship history.
- Are you building relationships consistently, or are most of your working hours spent on output that AI could produce?

---

## Part 2: Two specific fragilities (10 minutes)

From Part 1, identify two specific ways you are currently fragile to AI disruption.

Not categories of fragility — specific ones. Not "my writing could be automated" but "the type of briefing note I produce every week for the director is exactly the kind of structured summary that AI now produces in four minutes."

**For each fragility:**
- Name it specifically.
- Estimate how exposed you are: if this dimension of your work were automated tomorrow, how significant would the impact on your position be?
- Assess the timeline: is this a current risk, a twelve-month risk, or a three-to-five-year risk?

---

## Part 3: Two antifragile moves (10–15 minutes)

For each fragility you identified, design one concrete move that converts it from fragile to antifragile — using AI itself as part of the solution.

The antifragile move is not "get better at the thing AI is automating." That is robustness, not antifragility.

The antifragile move uses the disruption as leverage. It might look like:
- Becoming the person who understands what AI can and cannot do in this specific domain, making you more valuable as AI adoption accelerates.
- Redirecting the time freed by AI automation toward a skill or relationship that compounds over time.
- Positioning yourself at an intersection — two domains, two functions, two stakeholder groups — where your value grows as AI separates specialists from integrators.

**For each of your two fragilities:**
- Describe the antifragile move specifically.
- What would you need to do in the next ninety days to begin executing it?
- What would you need to stop doing to make room for it?

---

## Reflection (5 minutes)

Read back your Part 3 answers. Apply a simple test: if your company's AI access doubled tomorrow — more capable tools, freely available — would the moves you described in Part 3 make you more valuable, or less?

If the answer is "more valuable," you have identified genuine antifragile positions. If the answer is "it wouldn't matter much," your moves are in the robust category — survivable, but not compounding.

The goal is positions where AI improvement is your tailwind, not your headwind.

What are you going to start in the next thirty days?
