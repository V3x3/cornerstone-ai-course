## First-order thinking

First-order thinking asks: what is the immediate effect of this action?

"If I use AI to write emails, I'll write emails faster."

True. But incomplete.

## Second-order thinking

Second-order thinking asks: what happens as a consequence of the first-order effect?

"If everyone uses AI to write emails faster, inboxes fill faster. Reading becomes the bottleneck, not writing. The skill that matters shifts from composing to summarising and filtering."

Second-order thinking reveals the world after everyone has made the first-order decision.

## Applied to AI: three examples

**AI for writing:**
- First order: faster content creation
- Second order: content volume explodes, attention becomes scarce, trust in written content falls, differentiation shifts to voice and verification

**AI for coding:**
- First order: more code gets written faster
- Second order: debugging and maintaining AI-generated code becomes the constraint, software systems become more complex, the valuable skill shifts from writing code to reviewing and directing it

**AI for research:**
- First order: information retrieval is instant
- Second order: everyone has access to the same information, analysis quality and judgment become the differentiator, the premium moves to synthesis and recommendation, not information access

## What this tells you about where to invest

In each case, the first-order benefit gets competed away quickly. The second-order world reveals where the actual value will live.

Invest your learning in: the judgment, verification, and direction skills that remain scarce after AI handles the commodity work.

## Key takeaway

Second-order thinking is not pessimism — it's foresight. Ask not just "what does AI do?" but "what does the world look like when everyone uses AI the same way?" That's where your positioning decisions become meaningful.
