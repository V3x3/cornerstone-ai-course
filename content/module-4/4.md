In February 2020, a junior policy officer at a Brussels think tank was given an assignment nobody wanted.

The think tank's governance programme had a gap in its coverage of workplace policy. Remote work, specifically. The topic was considered minor — relevant perhaps for tech companies in Scandinavia, not a pressing priority for European governance research. The officer was three years into her career, not senior enough to decline the assignment, and not junior enough to ignore it. She spent January and February building a framework for what EU workplace policy should look like if remote work became significantly more common.

By March, she was the only person in the building who had thought seriously about what happens to labour law, social security systems, cross-border employment contracts, and works council requirements when workers are no longer physically present in one country.

She did not predict COVID. Nobody did. She was positioned — accidentally, because an under-resourced assignments manager needed someone to cover a minor topic — to benefit from an event that nobody saw coming.

By June, she was presenting to Commission officials. By September, she had been quoted in three policy papers. By December, she had two unsolicited job offers. She was promoted twice in eighteen months.

Black Swan positioning is about making that accident deliberate.

## What a Black Swan actually is

Nassim Taleb coined the term to describe rare, high-impact events that are retrospectively explainable but were not predictable in advance.

The key properties: the event is outside normal expectations based on prior experience. It carries an extreme impact. After it occurs, people construct explanations that make it seem obvious in retrospect.

COVID was a Black Swan to most of the world. Pandemics were not — epidemiologists had been warning about pandemic risk for decades. But the specific timing, geographic spread, and policy response was not predictable in advance with any useful precision.

The distinction Taleb draws — and it is the one that matters for careers — is between negative Black Swans and positive ones. Negative Black Swans destroy fragile positions. Positive Black Swans create enormous value for those positioned to capture them.

The Brussels policy officer's remote work framework was positioned for a positive Black Swan: an event that made her unusual knowledge suddenly valuable to a large number of people who needed it urgently.

## You cannot predict Black Swans. You can position for them.

The honest version of Taleb's insight is that it does not give you a prediction. It gives you a positioning strategy.

You cannot know which Black Swan is coming. You can build a position that benefits from a wide range of Black Swans, particularly in your professional domain. This requires two things: breadth across adjacent domains (so that no single unexpected development leaves you entirely irrelevant) and a few specific bets in areas that seem marginal now but have the structural properties of things that could become suddenly important.

The remote work topic had these properties. It was at the intersection of several major trends: digitisation, labour mobility, EU integration, changes in employment structure. It had been growing slowly for a decade. It had institutional actors — unions, employers, governments — with conflicting interests. The ingredients for a policy crisis were there. COVID was the catalyst that turned a slow-developing problem into an urgent one.

The policy officer didn't know COVID was coming. But she was working on a topic that had these structural characteristics, and that made her useful when the Black Swan arrived.

## EU institutions as a laboratory

The European institutional context produces a specific and recurring pattern: knowledge that seems niche and low-status becomes suddenly valuable when a political or regulatory crisis creates demand for it.

The official who knows both AI policy and agricultural subsidies looks like an odd combination until the EU proposes AI-based tools for CAP administration and suddenly every DG needs someone who can think about both simultaneously. The analyst who speaks Arabic and understands European trade policy looks like an unusual background until MENA trade relationships become politically critical. The lawyer who spent years on digital infrastructure regulation looks peripheral until a platform accountability crisis lands on the Commission's desk.

These are not predictable. They are, however, structural. The EU system produces policy crises at the intersection of multiple domains regularly. The people who benefit from those crises are the ones who happened to be developing knowledge at an intersection that became relevant.

The word "happened" is doing significant work in that sentence. For the policy officer in Brussels, it was genuinely accidental. For you, the goal is to reduce the role of accident.

## Building positive Black Swan exposure

Practical positioning involves two things: maintaining a few bets in areas that seem marginal but have the structural characteristics of things that could become important, and ensuring that your core position is robust enough that none of the bets destroy you if they don't pay off.

Within the AI domain specifically, there will be dozens of smaller Black Swans in the next five years: unexpected capability developments, regulatory shifts, specific applications that prove transformative in domains nobody expected, capability limitations that create demand for human judgment in specific contexts.

The professional with broad exposure across multiple AI-adjacent domains — policy, law, sector applications, governance, ethics, procurement — creates positive optionality across a wide range of these events. They will not benefit from all of them. They will benefit from some of them, and the ones they benefit from will likely be the ones nobody saw coming.

The professional who optimises purely for today's established applications of AI has a cleaner story and less apparent noise in their CV. They also have negative Black Swan exposure: if their specific domain shifts, they have nothing to fall back on and nothing positioned to benefit from the shift.

## The most valuable skill you'll have in five years

The most valuable professional skill you'll have in five years is probably something you haven't heard of yet. This is not speculation — it is the historical pattern of knowledge work disruption.

The professionals who built the most durable advantage during the internet era were not the ones who became expert in the specific applications that dominated in 2005. They were the ones who developed a genuine understanding of the underlying forces — what the internet changed about information asymmetry, about distribution, about trust — and could apply that understanding as specific applications evolved.

The same dynamic applies to AI. The specific applications that matter most in 2030 are not fully visible today. The underlying forces — what AI changes about knowledge work, about signalling, about organisational structure, about professional value — are visible now, and understanding them positions you for a wide range of specific developments.

Are you positioned to discover what matters in five years?

Or are you too busy optimising for today's game?
