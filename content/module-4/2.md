## Why game theory for AI?

AI adoption isn't a solo decision — it happens in a competitive environment. Your competitors, colleagues, and industry peers are all making adoption decisions simultaneously. Game theory is the branch of mathematics that models exactly this: strategic decisions among rational actors where outcomes depend on what others do.

## The Nash equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, given what everyone else is doing.

Simple example: if all your competitors are using AI for customer service and you aren't, you're at a disadvantage. If you adopt AI too, neither side is forced to maintain an expensive all-human operation. The equilibrium is mutual adoption.

Understanding this tells you: the question isn't "should I adopt AI?" but "what is the equilibrium state of my industry, and how far are we from it?"

## Technology arms races

Some AI adoption creates arms race dynamics. Everyone must adopt, not to gain advantage, but to avoid falling behind. The result: costs increase for everyone, the gap between leaders and followers remains, and the arms race consumes resources without producing a winner.

Identifying when you're in an arms race — versus a genuine leverage opportunity — is a strategic skill that pays off.

## Where you have actual advantage

Arms races reward: scale, capital, speed of adoption.

Genuine leverage comes from: applying AI to problems others haven't identified, combining AI capability with irreplaceable domain knowledge, or moving to a higher-value task after AI handles the lower one.

The professional who sees AI as the race misses the game. The one who uses AI to position for a better game wins.

## Key takeaway

Game theory tells you that AI adoption decisions aren't made in isolation — they're made in a strategic landscape. Understanding equilibria and arms races helps you choose where to compete, not just how to compete.
