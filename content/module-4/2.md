In 2019, an EU agency in Luxembourg restructured its communications department.

The rationale was the standard one: budget pressure, efficiency review, a new director who believed the existing team was too large for the agency's actual communications needs. Half the team was let go in a single month. The survivors received no additional support and roughly triple the workload.

For most of the team, the next two years were survival mode. They absorbed the work as best they could, maintained the existing publications calendar, and waited for conditions to stabilise.

One communications officer did something different.

She looked at the sudden workload and the absence of any established process to handle it, and saw a different kind of situation: a space where normal institutional inertia had been cleared away. The structures that previously constrained how communications work got done no longer existed. She used the chaos to completely redesign the department's workflow, build a network of external freelancers she could activate for surge work, and automate the routine reporting that had previously consumed a quarter of her week.

When AI tools arrived in 2023, she adopted them faster than anyone in the directorate. She had already been through a disruption and come out structurally different from it. The new tools integrated into a workflow that was already built for adaptation. Her colleagues who had survived the 2019 restructuring had returned, as soon as conditions stabilised, to doing what they had done before — only more of it. When AI arrived, they had nothing to show for the crisis.

She did.

## The Hydra and what it actually means

The Hydra is the nine-headed serpent from Greek mythology. Cut off one head, and two grow back. The mythological detail that matters is not the regeneration — it's the mechanism. The Hydra doesn't simply return to its original state after being cut. Each attack makes it more dangerous than before.

This is the distinction Nassim Taleb draws between resilience and antifragility. Resilience means returning to the original state after disruption. Antifragility means being transformed by disruption into something stronger than the original.

The Hydra professional is not the one who survives disruptions and bounces back. She is the one who looks at a disruption and asks: what does this open up that was not available before? What constraints have been removed? What space has been cleared?

This requires a specific cognitive move that does not come naturally: treating disruption as information about opportunity rather than threat. Most people, when faced with professional disruption, focus on what they are losing. The Hydra question is what is becoming accessible.

## Why EU institutions amplify the advantage

Institutional environments are, by design, disruption-averse. EU institutions maintain process continuity across political cycles, staff transitions, and external pressures. This is partly a feature — institutional memory, procedural consistency, and democratic accountability all benefit from stability. It is also, incidentally, an environment where anyone willing to move toward disruption faces almost no competition.

In a startup, every disruption attracts ten people trying to capitalise on it. In an EU institution, most people are moving in the opposite direction — toward the familiar, the procedural, the established. When disruption hits, the corridors empty of initiative. Everyone retreats to their job description.

This means the person who moves toward the disruption faces a vacuum. The communications officer in Luxembourg was not exceptionally talented. She was willing to treat a crisis as an experiment at a moment when nobody else was. In an organisation of three hundred people, she was the only one doing it.

This is a structural advantage that exists in most institutional environments, not just EU agencies. The more change-resistant the culture, the larger the opportunity for the person willing to engage with change directly.

## Becoming the AI person

The specific disruption available right now in institutional contexts is AI.

In most EU institutions, public sector bodies, and large European corporates, the majority of staff are in one of two positions relative to AI: they are ignoring it, or they are anxious about it.

The ignoring position is comfortable until it isn't. The anxious position is understandable but produces no advantage.

The Hydra position is to become, right now — while everyone else is still in the ignoring or anxious phase — the person who actually understands what AI can and cannot do in your specific institutional context. Not by becoming an AI engineer. Not by reading AI research papers. But by using the tools, documenting what works and what doesn't, understanding where the governance gaps are, and developing genuine practical knowledge about AI's application to the specific work of your organisation.

This is available to anyone. It requires time and attention, not technical background. The communications officer's competitive advantage in 2019 was not that she was better at workflow design than her colleagues. It was that she was doing workflow design when nobody else was, in a moment when the institutional structures that normally constrain such work had temporarily dissolved.

The AI disruption has temporarily dissolved institutional constraints in a different way: there is no established process for how to use AI, no official guidance on what it should be used for, no evaluation framework for AI-assisted output. This is a bureaucratic vacuum. Vacuums fill. The question is who fills them.

## The compounding dynamic

The reason the Hydra principle matters for careers, rather than just for individual disruptions, is compounding.

Each disruption the Luxembourg officer navigated made her better at navigating the next one. The 2019 restructuring built her capability for workflow redesign and external collaboration. That capability made AI adoption faster in 2023. The AI adoption built her reputation as an operational innovator. That reputation will make the next disruption — whatever it is — a source of opportunity rather than threat.

This is the compounding dynamic that distinguishes Hydra careers from resilient ones. Resilient careers survive disruptions and return to the baseline. Hydra careers use each disruption as an input into the next, building a compounding advantage that grows over time.

The careers that look effortless from the outside — the person who always seems to land on their feet, who keeps getting more interesting work, whose reputation keeps growing without obvious explanation — are often the product of this dynamic, accumulated over a decade.

The last time your professional life was disrupted, did you come out stronger?

If not, what will you do differently this time?
