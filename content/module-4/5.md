An EU affairs director in Brussels has a career that looks, from the outside, like a straightforward accumulation of expertise.

He has spent fifteen years in competition law. DG COMP cases, state aid, merger control. Deep, specific, defensible expertise that European corporates pay significant fees to access. His reputation is solid. His network is genuine. His income is stable.

He also has a newsletter.

He started it six years ago because he was reading more about technology regulation than his job required him to, and he wanted somewhere to write down what he was thinking. AI and competition law. Platform liability. Digital markets regulation. The intersection of technology policy with the competition frameworks he knew intimately.

His colleagues saw it as a hobby. A diversion. Some thought it was slightly embarrassing — a senior professional writing opinion pieces on the internet.

He saw it differently. He was making a small, asymmetric bet: if technology regulation became a significant EU policy priority, six years of writing about the intersection of competition law and technology policy would be worth considerably more than the time it cost. The downside was limited — a few hours per week, some mild professional ribbing. The upside was unbounded — if the bet paid off, he would have a public profile, an audience, and a body of work that no competitor could quickly replicate.

Digital Markets Act. Digital Services Act. The EU AI Act. The bet paid off. He was quoted in briefings before final votes. Journalists found him through the newsletter. Two major clients came from people who had been reading for years.

His career is a barbell. He did not plan it as one. But when you understand the barbell strategy, you recognise what he built.

## The architecture of the barbell

Nassim Taleb's barbell strategy is, at its core, about asymmetry. The conventional approach to managing risk is diversification: spread your exposures so that no single outcome can be catastrophic. Moderate risk, moderate return, across many positions. This produces a smooth distribution of outcomes.

Taleb's critique is that this smooth distribution exposes you to the tail risks you haven't anticipated. When a Black Swan arrives, moderate diversification is not enough — everything that is moderately positioned gets hit moderately.

The barbell alternative: extreme conservatism on one end and extreme risk-taking on the other, with nothing in the middle. In investing, this means holding most of your assets in the safest instruments available and a small fraction in highly speculative positions with potentially enormous upside. The worst case is modest losses on the speculative end. The best case is transformative upside.

Applied to careers: maintain a safe, deep core expertise that is your reliable income and reputation base, and simultaneously take multiple small, asymmetric bets on emerging domains where the downside is limited — a few hours per week, modest personal investment — and the upside is potentially large.

The forbidden zone: the fragile middle. Medium-risk bets that don't threaten your core but don't open transformative possibilities either. Trying to be moderately good at six things, investing equally across them, hedging without conviction. This is the most common professional development strategy and the most fragile: it spreads your finite time and attention across positions that are neither safe enough to protect you nor bold enough to produce asymmetric returns.

## Why the middle is fragile

The middle is where most professional development spending concentrates. Executive education in adjacent leadership skills. Cross-functional project assignments. Professional certifications in established domains. Networking in your own industry.

These investments are not worthless. They maintain and marginally extend the core. The problem is that they rarely produce asymmetric returns — because they are concentrated in domains that are already well-developed, well-understood, and well-populated by competitors who have made the same investments.

The EU affairs director's newsletter was not a middle-ground investment. It was a bet in an area that was underdeveloped, outside his immediate domain, and most easily dismissed as peripheral. Its value came precisely from its peripherality. If he had chosen to invest the same time in deepening his competition law expertise — already well-developed — the return would have been modest and predictable. The newsletter was outside his core, which is why it produced asymmetric returns when the bet paid off.

## AI enables both ends of the barbell

AI is particularly useful for maintaining both ends of the strategy.

On the safe end: AI helps you reinforce and deepen your core expertise more efficiently. Research faster, stay current on developments across a large body of case law, draft work product that frees time for the higher-judgment activities. The core gets more robust with less effort.

On the risky end: AI makes exploration of unfamiliar domains dramatically cheaper. One hour per week of structured AI-assisted learning in a completely unfamiliar area — asking AI to explain the major debates, the key actors, the historical context, the current developments — produces more working knowledge than a month of reading might have five years ago.

The combination: use AI to maintain and strengthen your core with less time investment, and redirect the time saved to the exploratory bets that represent the risky end of the barbell.

The forbidden use: using AI to be slightly more efficient at your current job without redirecting the saved time toward anything asymmetric. This is the path to the fragile middle at higher speed.

## How to identify your barbell bets

The best barbell bets share characteristics that are worth identifying before you invest.

**Structural under-investment:** an area where most people in your domain are not building knowledge, despite evidence that it is becoming relevant. Technology regulation was this for competition lawyers in 2018. AI governance is this for many policy professionals today.

**Low downside:** the investment required is modest enough that being wrong is not catastrophic. A newsletter, a few hours per week of reading, a side project. Not a career pivot.

**Convex upside:** if the bet pays off, the returns are not proportional to the investment — they are disproportionate. The EU affairs director's newsletter did not pay off proportionally to his time investment. It paid off disproportionately because it produced a specific positioning that was suddenly very valuable and that nobody else had.

**Your specific insight:** the best bets are ones where you have a genuine perspective that is not widely shared. The director's newsletter worked partly because he was writing about technology regulation through the lens of competition law — a combination that was specific to him and therefore not crowded.

This module has covered the frameworks for antifragile careers: understanding where you are fragile, building toward disruption rather than away from it, developing optionality, positioning for Black Swans. The barbell is how you structure all of this practically.

Look at how you spent your professional development time this month. Was it all in the safe middle?

The barbell demands something that feels risky. Not reckless — just uncomfortable enough that it might change your trajectory.
