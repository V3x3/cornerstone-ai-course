## What is via negativa?

Via negativa is a Latin phrase meaning "the negative way." In philosophy and theology, it means defining something by what it is *not*, rather than what it is.

Nassim Taleb applies it to decision-making: often, the best improvements come not from adding things but from removing them. Subtracting noise, bad habits, misleading heuristics, and cognitive clutter.

## Why subtraction is harder than addition

We are addition-biased. When something isn't working, we try to fix it by doing more — more effort, more tools, more information. The surgeon who removes the tumour is celebrated less than the one who performs a complex intervention, even if removal was the right call.

Applied to thinking with AI: the temptation is to add more prompts, more context, more steps. Often the improvement comes from removing the noise that corrupts the output.

## Via negativa applied to AI use

**Remove: prompt clutter.** More words in a prompt are not always better. Identify the core instruction and cut everything else. Precision beats volume.

**Remove: over-reliance on AI for judgment calls.** When you offload too many decisions to AI, you erode the domain expertise that makes your prompts good in the first place. Keep the judgment layer. Remove the tedium.

**Remove: the assumption that the first output is the answer.** The first draft is data. The feedback loop is where thinking happens. Remove the habit of accepting output uncritically.

**Remove: tools that create friction.** The best AI workflow is the one you'll actually use. If a tool has too many steps, remove steps. Simplicity is a feature.

## The discipline

Via negativa is a discipline, not a technique. It requires resisting the pull toward complexity — in tools, in prompts, in processes. The question is always: what can I remove without losing essential function?

## Key takeaway

Addition is intuitive. Subtraction is the rarer skill. Via negativa applied to your AI workflow means asking not "what should I add?" but "what can I remove to make this clearer?" Often the answer is: more than you think.
