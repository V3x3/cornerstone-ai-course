Three EU policy officers in the same Brussels directorate hear the same rumour on the same afternoon: the Commission is piloting an AI system to draft preliminary impact assessments. Results are promising. Wider rollout is possible within eighteen months.

Officer A has spent six years building her career around exactly this work. Impact assessments are sixty percent of her visible output. She goes home that evening and lies awake.

Officer B is less concerned. She has a law degree and has kept her legal drafting skills current. If impact assessments shift to AI, she can pivot to legal review. She keeps working.

Officer C does something different. The next morning, she asks to be included in the pilot programme. By the end of the month, she's the only person in the directorate who understands both what the AI system produces and where its output breaks down. She starts documenting its failure modes. She writes an internal note about governance gaps that her director forwards to the cabinet.

Same stressor. Same information. Three completely different outcomes.

Officer A is fragile. Officer B is robust. Officer C is antifragile. The disruption made Officer C more valuable than she was before it arrived.

## Taleb's triad

Nassim Taleb introduced a taxonomy that most career advice ignores because it complicates the conventional wisdom.

Fragile things break under stress. You already understand this. Your job description as it exists today, if unchanged, is fragile to technological disruption. This is the category most people think they are not in — and most people are wrong about that.

Robust things survive stress. They neither benefit nor suffer significantly from volatility. Robustness is the goal most career advice aims for: diversify your skills, build a safety net, develop a range of capabilities so no single disruption can eliminate you. This is sensible. It is also insufficient.

Antifragile things gain from stress. The word is awkward precisely because we don't have a better one — we lack vocabulary for the concept because we rarely see it clearly. The antifragile is not just resilient; it requires the disruption to become more valuable. Remove the stressor and the antifragile thing is merely average. Apply the stressor and it grows stronger.

The distinction matters because most professionals optimise for robustness and call it safety. In a stable environment, robustness is enough. In an environment of persistent disruption — which is what AI is creating — robustness survives but doesn't win. Antifragility wins.

## The convexity requirement

Taleb's technical description of antifragility involves convexity: the upside from disruption must be larger than the downside. This is not just a property you either have or don't — it is something you can engineer.

Officer C engineered convexity by positioning herself at the intersection of AI capability and human institutional knowledge. The AI pilot could produce an impact assessment, but it had no idea how the Commissioner's cabinet would receive it, what the Parliament rapporteur's red lines were, or which member state had a blocking minority forming around a specific provision. Officer C knew those things. The AI made those things more valuable by automating everything around them.

Her upside from AI adoption was large: she became essential. Her downside was minimal: in the worst case, she had learned a new technology and documented its limitations — useful regardless of what happened next.

Officer A's convexity was inverted: her upside from AI adoption was negligible (perhaps she could produce assessments slightly faster), while her downside was severe (her primary value proposition could be replicated by a system).

Before you assess whether you are fragile, robust, or antifragile, assess whether your current position is convex or concave with respect to AI improvement. If AI getting better makes you less valuable, you are in a concave position. If AI getting better makes you more valuable — because it amplifies the skills you have that AI cannot replicate — your position is convex.

## EU institutions as a laboratory

The European institutional context is particularly instructive here because it contains, in concentrated form, all three positions of the Talbottian triad.

The translator who built a career on producing accurate French-to-English renderings of Commission documents is in a fragile position. DeepL and similar tools have already eroded this market. Machine translation is not perfect, but it is good enough for a large fraction of the work, and it is improving. The remaining value is in judgment — which translations require human nuance, which can be reviewed at speed — not in the translation itself.

The policy analyst who has learned to use AI tools but hasn't changed her fundamental approach is robust. She processes information faster. Her core skill — understanding how EU institutions work, who the players are, what the political constraints are — is not directly threatened. She will survive.

The analyst who uses AI to process ten times more stakeholder input, synthesising across dozens of languages and consultation documents to surface the pattern that matters, is antifragile. She can now do something that was impossible without AI. More importantly, as AI improves, her capability improves — the tool she's built her position around gets more powerful. Her value compounds with AI adoption rather than being eroded by it.

## The honest self-assessment

Most people, if asked whether they are fragile, will say no. They are wrong about themselves in a specific, consistent way: they confuse the current stability of their position with its fundamental robustness to change.

Officer A's position felt robust for six years. Nothing disrupted it. The absence of disruption was not evidence of antifragility — it was evidence that the disruption hadn't arrived yet.

The right question is not "has anything happened to threaten my position?" but "what is my position's structure if something does?"

Think specifically about what you produce professionally. What fraction of it could be generated, at adequate quality, by AI tools available today? What fraction requires judgment, relationship, institutional knowledge, or accountability that AI cannot replicate? Is the latter fraction growing or shrinking?

"Resilience" is the most dangerous word in career advice. It means you survive. It doesn't mean you win.

Which one are you actually building?
