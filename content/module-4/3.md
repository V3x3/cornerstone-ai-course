A mid-career policy advisor at the EIB in Luxembourg has spent seven years building deep expertise in renewable energy project finance. She knows the sector: the regulatory frameworks, the major actors, the financing instruments, the political dynamics across member states. Her knowledge is genuine and hard-won.

It is also narrow.

If the EIB shifts its strategic priorities away from renewables — which it has done before, and will do again — she has one skill in a market that may be contracting. Her value is concentrated in a single domain, which means her professional safety is inversely correlated with the health of that domain.

A colleague with the same tenure at the same institution looks different. He has developed working knowledge of renewables, yes — but also digital infrastructure financing, which he explored during a task force assignment in year four. And climate adaptation financing, which he studied independently after a senior colleague mentioned it was becoming politically relevant. He is less deep in any individual area than she is. He has three viable career paths where she has one.

When the EIB announces a strategic reorientation toward climate adaptation in year eight, he has three applications to consider. She has one, and it is to the team being restructured.

This is the difference between expertise and optionality. Both are valuable. Expertise without optionality is fragility with a good CV.

## The option as a concept

In finance, an option is the right, but not the obligation, to take a course of action — to buy a stock at a certain price, to exercise a contract, to pursue an opportunity. Options have a specific property: they cap your downside (you don't have to exercise them if conditions are unfavourable) while leaving the upside open.

Taleb applies this concept to careers and decision-making more broadly. Optionality means maintaining multiple viable paths so that no single disruption can trap you. It is the antifragile version of career planning: instead of optimising for a single path and hoping it remains viable, you build a position where disruption is more likely to open new paths than to close the only one you have.

The professional who has cultivated expertise and optionality can choose to exercise the option of the new path or to stay in the current one. The professional who has only expertise has no option — they either succeed in their current domain or lose the game.

## The specialisation trap in institutional contexts

European institutional systems — EU agencies, public ministries, large regulated firms — systematically reward specialisation. Grade structures, performance frameworks, and career ladders are built around depth. The expert is valued more than the generalist. This is, in most contexts, sensible.

The trap is that these systems reward specialisation at the level of current domain, which is static, when the career environment is dynamic. The renewable energy expert's seven years of depth are enormously valuable — until the domain contracts or AI makes a significant fraction of the technical analysis she produces accessible to anyone with a good prompt.

AI accelerates this dynamic. It makes any individual domain of expertise more accessible, which shifts the premium from depth to the ability to connect across domains. The policy advisor who can work in renewables *and* digital infrastructure *and* climate adaptation — and can connect insights across those areas — produces something that AI-augmented specialists cannot: genuine cross-domain synthesis that draws on relationships, institutional memory, and pattern recognition accumulated across multiple contexts.

Depth still matters. But depth without breadth is increasingly fragile.

## AI as an optionality engine

Here is the specific opportunity: AI makes building breadth dramatically cheaper than it has ever been.

Ten years ago, developing working knowledge in an adjacent domain required months of reading, courses, mentorship, or direct experience. The investment was substantial enough that most professionals made one depth bet and maintained it. Building the breadth the colleague in Luxembourg had required sustained investment over years.

AI changes this. One hour of structured AI-assisted exploration in an adjacent domain — specific questions, follow-up questions, requests to explain implications and connections, requests to map the major actors and the major debates — produces more working knowledge than a week of reading might have five years ago.

This does not mean AI replaces experience. Deep expertise still requires the thing AI cannot provide: actual work in the domain, relationships built over time, pattern recognition from navigating real situations. But working knowledge — the kind of familiarity that lets you follow an expert conversation, identify the interesting questions, and recognise where your skills are potentially applicable — is now much cheaper to develop.

The practical implication: the professional who spends twenty percent of their AI time exploring domains adjacent to their core expertise is building optionality at almost no cost. The professional who uses all of their AI time to go deeper in their existing domain is improving their current position while inadvertently narrowing their future options.

## The 20% rule in practice

The specific allocation — spending roughly twenty percent of AI time on adjacent domains — is a starting point, not a formula. The point is directional: you should be using AI to expand your option set, not just to deepen your current position.

Adjacent domains worth exploring are those that: share institutional context with your current work (the renewable energy specialist exploring climate adaptation is not starting from zero — the regulatory and financing frameworks overlap significantly); are growing in political or institutional salience (the analyst who started learning AI governance policy two years ago is in a different position today than one who started last month); or represent a skill gap that has recurred in your work (the policy specialist who keeps wishing she understood more quantitative modelling has an obvious adjacency to develop).

The exploration does not need to be systematic or formal. A few hours of AI-assisted reading in an unfamiliar domain, a conversation with a colleague from a different team that AI helps you prepare for, a policy paper from an adjacent area that you ask AI to explain and connect to your own work. The goal is not to become an expert — it is to be a non-expert who can follow the conversation, identify the interesting questions, and recognise the connections.

Expertise is what you know. Optionality is what you *could* know.

AI makes the second one nearly free.

Are you using it?
