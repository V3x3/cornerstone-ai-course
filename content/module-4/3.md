## The prisoner's dilemma

Two suspects are arrested. Each must choose, without communicating: confess or stay silent.

- If both stay silent: both get a short sentence.
- If both confess: both get a longer sentence.
- If one confesses and the other stays silent: the one who confessed goes free; the silent one gets the longest sentence.

The rational choice for each individual is to confess — regardless of what the other does. The result: both confess and both are worse off than if they'd cooperated.

Individual rationality produces collective irrationality. This is the dilemma.

## The automation version

Replace "confess" with "automate aggressively" and "stay silent" with "maintain human-centred work."

- If one organisation automates while others don't: competitive advantage for the automator.
- If everyone automates simultaneously: the cost advantages are competed away, but the transition costs are paid by everyone.
- If no one automates: equilibrium, but also the first mover who breaks ranks wins.

The competitive pressure pushes every player toward automation — even when the collective outcome (unemployment spikes, social instability, skills erosion) is worse for everyone.

## Why this matters for your choices

You're not just a passive observer in this dynamic — you're a player. Understanding the structure of the game helps you make choices that aren't purely reactive.

Options that escape the dilemma:
- **Move up the value chain** before automation reaches your role
- **Become the person who directs the automation**, not the one it replaces
- **Advocate for coordination mechanisms** (policy, industry standards) that slow down the race in ways that benefit workers and organisations alike

## Key takeaway

The automation dilemma is real. Individual rationality points toward maximum automation; collective rationality requires coordination. Knowing the game structure doesn't eliminate the pressure — but it tells you where to focus your energy.
