## Why most people's AI usage doesn't stick

They try AI for a few tasks, get inconsistent results, and drift back to their old tools. The problem isn't the AI — it's the lack of a system.

A workflow is a repeatable process applied to a recurring task. You don't need to design one for everything. You need one for the three things you do most.

## Step 1: Identify your highest-leverage recurring tasks

Ask yourself: what do I do every week that takes time and feels repetitive?

Common answers:
- Writing emails and responses
- Summarising long documents or meetings
- Researching topics you're not expert in
- Drafting reports, proposals, or briefs
- Preparing for meetings (research, agenda, questions)

Pick one. That's where you build your first workflow.

## Step 2: Design the prompt pattern

For your chosen task, design a standard prompt structure you can reuse:

```
Context: [who you are, what organisation, what the task is for]
Task: [what you want the AI to do]
Input: [paste the raw material here]
Format: [how you want the output structured]
Constraints: [what to avoid, tone, length]
```

You don't need all five fields every time. But having a template means you're not reinventing the prompt every time you sit down.

## Step 3: Build it into your existing tools

The workflow you'll actually use is the one with the least friction. Options:

- Save prompt templates in a notes app (Notion, Obsidian, Apple Notes)
- Use Claude's Projects feature to store a persistent system prompt for your context
- Build a simple text shortcut that expands to your base prompt

## Step 4: Iterate, don't perfect

Run the workflow 10 times. After each run, note what didn't work. Tweak the prompt. After 10 uses, you'll have something genuinely good — and you built it from real experience, not theory.

## Key takeaway

The best AI workflow is simple, anchored to something you already do, and gets better each time you use it. Start with one task, build the template, use it until it works.
