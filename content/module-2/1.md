## What is prompt engineering?

Prompting is how you communicate with an AI. Prompt engineering is the practice of structuring those communications to get better results. It's not magic — it's applied precision.

## Zero-shot prompting

You give the model a task with no examples. You rely entirely on the model's trained knowledge to interpret what you want.

**Example:** "Summarise this article in three bullet points."

Works well for: common tasks the model has seen millions of examples of in training. Fails for: unusual formats, domain-specific outputs, tasks requiring a specific structure.

## Few-shot prompting

You provide 1–3 examples of the input-output pattern you want before giving the real task.

**Example:**
```
Input: Q3 revenue increased 12%. EBITDA margin improved to 18%.
Output: Strong quarter — revenue up, margins improving.

Input: Customer churn rose to 8.2%. Net promoter score dropped 14 points.
Output: Warning signs — retention and satisfaction both declining.

Input: [your actual data here]
Output:
```

The model learns the format and tone from your examples. This dramatically improves consistency for structured tasks.

## Chain-of-thought prompting

For complex reasoning tasks, you instruct the model to show its work. This forces it to reason step by step rather than jumping to a conclusion.

**Add to any prompt:** "Think step by step before answering."

Why it works: the model's intermediate steps serve as a kind of working memory. Breaking the problem into steps reduces the chance of reasoning errors.

Use for: multi-step problems, logical analysis, anything where you want to inspect the reasoning, not just the answer.

## A practical hierarchy

1. Try zero-shot first — it's fastest
2. If the format isn't right, add 2–3 examples (few-shot)
3. If the reasoning is wrong, add chain-of-thought

## Key takeaway

Prompting is a skill. Zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought are your three core techniques. Use them deliberately, not randomly.
