## What we actually have: narrow AI

Today's AI — including GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — is narrow AI. It is extraordinarily good at language tasks, and increasingly capable at reasoning and coding. But it cannot:

- Learn permanently from a conversation
- Transfer a skill from one domain to another without retraining
- Act autonomously in the physical world without explicit tools

It's narrow in the sense that its capabilities, however impressive, are bounded.

## What AGI would mean

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to a system that can perform any intellectual task a human can — with the same flexibility and adaptability. It would generalise across domains, learn from single examples, and improve itself.

No one has built this. We don't know when — or whether — it will be built.

## Why the headlines are unreliable

AI progress attracts hyperbole in both directions. Enthusiasts announce AGI is weeks away. Sceptics say it's impossible. Neither camp has a reliable track record.

What we can observe:
- Current models are improving rapidly
- Each generation is more capable than the last
- The jump from narrow to general intelligence is not yet understood

## A practical stance

For your working life: treat AI as a powerful narrow tool. It will become more powerful. Plan for that. But base your decisions on what it can actually do today, not on predictions about what it might do in five years.

## Key takeaway

AGI is a real research goal, not a current reality. The AI tools you use today are narrow — exceptional at language, reasoning, and synthesis, but not general. Understanding this helps you set accurate expectations and avoid both hype and dismissal.
