## The core mechanism

A language model does one thing: given a sequence of tokens, it predicts the most likely next token. That's it. No understanding. No reasoning. Just pattern matching at scale.

And yet — from this simple operation emerges something that can write legal briefs, explain quantum mechanics, and hold a coherent conversation across dozens of exchanges.

## What is a token?

A token is roughly a word, or part of a word. "Antifragile" is one token. "unbelievable" might be two tokens: "unbeliev" + "able". The model doesn't see letters — it sees chunks of text that it has assigned meaning to during training.

## Probability distributions

When you type "The capital of France is", the model doesn't know the answer. It has seen that sequence (or similar ones) millions of times in its training data, and "Paris" appears after it overwhelmingly often. So "Paris" gets a very high probability, and the model outputs it.

This matters because:
- The model is **always guessing** — it just guesses very well
- Unusual or rare facts are more likely to be wrong because the training signal was weaker
- The model has no sense of "truth" — only of what text tends to follow other text

## Why this is actually profound

You might think "just pattern matching" is dismissive. It isn't. Human language encodes millennia of human thought. If you train on enough of it, you don't just learn words — you learn the structures of reasoning, the shapes of arguments, the logic of scientific explanation.

The model has learned, through prediction, what good thinking looks like. That's why it can help you think better.

## Key takeaway

LLMs predict text, not truth. This makes them powerful collaborators for drafting, reasoning, and exploring ideas — but unreliable sources of verified facts. Use them accordingly.
