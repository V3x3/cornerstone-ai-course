## Where AI actually came from

Before large language models existed, AI worked through rules. Programmers wrote explicit logic: *if the input contains "hello", respond with "hi"*. These systems were brittle — they could only handle exactly what was anticipated.

## The statistical turn

In the 1990s and 2000s, researchers began training models on data rather than hand-coding rules. Feed the model millions of sentences, let it learn patterns statistically. This worked for narrow tasks — spam detection, basic translation — but hit a ceiling.

## The transformer breakthrough (2017)

In 2017, a Google research paper called *"Attention Is All You Need"* introduced the transformer architecture. The key innovation: **attention mechanisms** that let a model weigh the relevance of every word in a sentence against every other word, simultaneously.

This was the unlock. Models could now understand context across long passages, not just word by word.

## What this means for you

Every tool you use today — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — is built on transformers. Understanding this helps you:

- Know *why* these models are good at language but not calculation
- Understand why longer context is harder to maintain
- Predict what kinds of tasks they'll handle reliably vs. poorly

## Key takeaway

AI didn't become powerful because someone wrote smarter rules. It became powerful because we stopped writing rules and started training on patterns. That shift changes everything about how you should use it.
