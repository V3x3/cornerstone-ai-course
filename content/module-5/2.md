In May 1997, Gary Kasparov lost to Deep Blue, and the reaction was predictable: chess was over. Machines had beaten the best human player in history. What was the point?

What actually happened was more interesting.

Within a few years, "advanced chess" — human and machine teams playing together — emerged and revealed something unexpected. Human plus machine teams consistently outperformed both pure humans and pure machines. Not because they averaged the two, but because the combination was genuinely stronger than either component.

The mechanism, when researchers studied it carefully, was specific: the advantage only held when the human player knew which decisions to keep. A human who deferred to the machine on every move added nothing — the team performed at machine level, with the human as a passenger. A human who overrode the machine on every move negated the computational advantage — the team performed at human level, with the machine unused.

The centaur advantage — the genuine super-human performance — came from humans who had developed precise judgment about when to trust the machine and when to override it. Which positions required calculation the machine handled better? Which positions required strategic intuition the human retained? Which machine recommendations were sound in isolation but missed a psychological dynamic that would matter ten moves later?

This is the central professional skill of the AI era, and it is not a technical skill. It is a judgment skill about the nature of your own work.

## What the centaur model requires

The centaur model has a simple structure but a demanding requirement.

The structure: the human retains strategic control over which tasks to delegate and which to retain. The requirement: this delegation decision must be an active judgment, not a default.

Most people who use AI are not centaurs. They are passengers who have not noticed. The delegation happened gradually — each task slightly higher stakes than the last, the AI performing adequately each time, the human's review becoming lighter because nothing went wrong. By the time something does go wrong, the human has lost the ability to catch the error because they have stopped exercising the capacity that would detect it.

The transition from centaur to passenger is invisible from the inside. It feels like efficiency. It registers as productivity. Nothing announces it.

The test for whether you are a centaur or a passenger is not whether you use AI extensively — centaurs often use AI more than passengers. The test is whether you can articulate, for any given AI use, exactly why you accepted the output. Not "it looked right" — but a specific account of the judgment you applied in deciding this output was correct and could be acted on.

If you cannot articulate that account, you didn't make a decision. You outsourced one. The output may be correct. But you are not accountable for it in the way a centaur is — and in most professional contexts, accountability is not optional.

## The delegation framework

The most useful heuristic for centaur delegation: retain judgment, delegate execution.

Judgment means: which problem are we solving, what counts as a good solution, what are the constraints, and when is this decision consequential enough to require slow thinking. Execution means: produce the analysis, draft the document, process the data, surface the options.

AI is excellent at execution. It is poor at judgment — not because it lacks capability in any simple sense, but because judgment requires accountability that AI cannot bear and context that AI does not have.

The specific line between judgment and execution varies by domain. In fiscal policy modelling, a senior advisor in the French Ministry of Economy might delegate the scenario generation (execution) while retaining the determination of which scenarios are politically realistic, which assumptions require stress-testing, and which outputs should actually be presented to the minister (judgment). The AI produces fifty scenarios. She decides which three matter.

In legal work, a junior attorney might delegate initial case research and precedent summary (execution) while retaining the determination of which precedents are actually analogous to the specific facts at hand and what the argumentation strategy should be (judgment). The AI finds cases. She builds the argument.

In communications work, a policy officer might delegate the first draft and the translation (execution) while retaining the determination of tone, political framing, and which stakeholders' concerns need specific attention (judgment). The AI writes. She decides what gets said.

The framework breaks down when execution is mistaken for judgment, or when the execution output is accepted without the judgment step being performed. This is the outsourcing trap from the previous lesson — not a failure of the model but a failure to apply it.

## The centaur test

The centaur test is simple: if you could not explain why you accepted AI's output to a sceptical, knowledgeable colleague who would push back on your reasoning, you did not make a centaur decision.

This test matters because it requires you to have had the reasoning in the first place. A passenger accepts AI output and moves on. A centaur accepts AI output because they have formed a specific judgment that the output is correct, adequate, or at least acceptable given the stakes — and they can say why.

The sceptical colleague is important. Explaining your decision to someone who already agrees with you does not require rigour. Explaining it to someone who will challenge it does.

Apply this test to your last ten AI interactions. For how many of them could you produce a clear, specific account of why you accepted the output? Not "I checked it" — checked it how, against what standard, with what result? Not "it looked right" — what specifically looked right, and what would have made it look wrong?

Most professionals who are honest about this test will find that a significant fraction of their AI use is passenger use, not centaur use. This is not a reason to panic. It is information about where the risk is concentrated.

## Recalibrating

The centaur model is not an argument for doing more work manually. It is an argument for being deliberate about which work you maintain control of and which you genuinely delegate.

Some tasks can be fully delegated. If the stakes are low and the output is easy to verify, full delegation is often correct. The question is whether you are making that decision explicitly or by default.

The recalibration is simple: once a week, identify one AI task where you are currently a passenger and decide either to apply genuine judgment to it going forward, or to explicitly accept that delegation is appropriate given the stakes.

Over time, this practice builds a more accurate map of where you are a centaur and where you are a passenger. The map is the starting point for fixing the parts that need fixing.

A centaur wins because the human knows when to override the machine.

When was the last time you overrode your AI?

If you cannot remember, you might not be the centaur.
