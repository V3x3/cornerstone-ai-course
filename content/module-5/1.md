A civil servant in the Polish Ministry of Digital Affairs has been using AI for six months to draft policy briefings. She is forty percent faster. Her manager is impressed. She is impressing herself.

What neither of them has noticed: she no longer reads the source documents.

The workflow developed gradually, almost without her noticing. First she'd read the documents and then use AI to help structure her thoughts. Then she'd skim the documents and let AI synthesise the key points. Then she'd feed the documents to AI and review the summary. By month four, the review was light. By month six, it was largely cursory — checking that the format looked right, adjusting a sentence or two.

Last week, AI summarised a two-hundred-page stakeholder consultation report and missed a dissenting opinion from a key industry association. The opinion was buried on page forty-seven, phrased in the cautious, hedged language that associations use when they want something on record but don't want to appear obstructionist. It wasn't the most prominent finding. It also wasn't a footnote — it represented a constituency with direct access to the ministry's supervising committee.

She didn't catch it because she didn't read the report. She didn't read the report because AI was supposed to. The briefing went to the minister. The dissenting opinion surfaced two weeks later in a parliamentary hearing. A committee member asked why the ministry's briefing had not addressed the industry association's concerns.

Nobody blamed AI. Everybody blamed her.

## What cognitive offloading actually does

The research on cognitive offloading is substantial and the finding is consistent: using an external tool to perform a cognitive task reduces your ability to perform that task without the tool.

This is not a controversial claim. It is documented across many domains. GPS use reduces spatial memory — people who use navigation systems regularly perform worse on tasks requiring them to recall or construct mental maps of environments they've navigated. Calculator use reduces facility with mental arithmetic. Spell-checkers reduce the attention paid to spelling during composition.

The pattern is uniform: when a tool reliably handles a task, the brain stops maintaining the neural infrastructure for doing it independently. This is not stupidity or laziness — it is basic cognitive efficiency. Why maintain capacity you're not using?

The problem is when the tool fails. GPS gets you to the wrong address in a place you don't know, and you have no internal map to fall back on. The calculator dies during an exam. The AI misses the dissenting opinion on page forty-seven.

The trade-off is asymmetric in a way that makes it easy to miss. The efficiency gain from cognitive offloading is immediate and visible: the civil servant is forty percent faster, and her manager notices. The capability loss is gradual and invisible — until it manifests in exactly the situation where it matters most.

## Why government is the extreme case

Every knowledge worker faces this trade-off. Government makes it extreme.

In consulting, the cost of missing a nuance is a client who is less impressed than they might have been. In government, the cost of missing a nuance can be legislation that creates perverse incentives, a regulatory gap that an industry actor exploits for years, or a minister publicly embarrassed before a parliamentary committee.

The stakes also concentrate in documents. Government policy is made through text — consultation responses, impact assessments, legal opinions, briefing notes. The quality of a policy depends on the quality of the reading that informs it. When AI does the reading, the quality of the policy depends on the quality of the AI's reading — which is good but not perfect, and whose imperfections are not randomly distributed but follow systematic patterns that the human reader would need to understand to catch.

The civil servant's error wasn't using AI for a two-hundred-page document. It was using AI without maintaining the ability to catch AI's mistakes. Those are different things.

## The framework for honest delegation

For every task you delegate to AI, there is a cognitive skill you are no longer exercising. The question is not whether this trade-off exists — it always does — but whether it is acceptable given what is at stake.

Some tasks are safe to fully delegate. If AI drafts a first version of meeting minutes and you review them, you are offloading the initial transcription while retaining the judgment. Your ability to do meeting minutes independently is not critical to your professional function. The trade-off is fine.

Other tasks are not safe to fully delegate. Reading the source documents is one of them. Not because AI cannot summarise — it can, often well. But because the act of reading is also the act of attending to what doesn't fit the summary. Anomalies, dissenting voices, and ambiguously phrased concerns are exactly the things that summarisation algorithms deprioritise. They appear rarely, but they have disproportionate consequences.

The civil servant's workflow had evolved into a situation where she was fully dependent on AI for the most consequential part of her job without being aware of it. This is the outsourcing trap: delegation begins as efficiency and ends as dependency, and the transition is invisible because the tool works well enough, most of the time, that the dependency never announces itself.

## The diagnostic question

There is a simple test. For any task where you currently use AI: if your AI access were revoked tomorrow, could you perform this task at the quality your role requires?

If the answer is yes, you have leverage. If the answer is uncertain, you have a risk. If the answer is no, and you haven't made a deliberate decision to accept that dependency, you have a problem.

The civil servant would have failed this test for the core skill of her job — close reading of policy documents — without ever having decided that was acceptable.

You're faster with AI. Are you also worse?

If you don't know the answer, that's the answer.
