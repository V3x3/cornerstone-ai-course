Two directors at the same EU member state's finance ministry both use AI for exactly ten hours per week.

Director A uses it to draft ministerial correspondence, summarise meeting notes, and format quarterly reports. The output is consistent. Her team appreciates the turnaround speed. Her manager has noticed that documents come back from her faster than before.

Director B uses it to explore fiscal policy approaches in countries she has never studied — Scandinavian property tax structures, Irish corporate tax reform, Portuguese housing policy experiments. She uses it to simulate how proposed tax changes might affect different demographic segments under different economic scenarios. She uses it to stress-test her department's core assumptions against economic models from heterodox schools of thought that she would never have time to engage with directly.

Both log ten hours of AI use. Both would say they use AI extensively. One is treading water. The other is building a compounding advantage that will widen over the next five years.

The hours are identical. The returns are asymmetric.

## The 2×2 that explains the difference

The difference between Director A and Director B can be mapped on two dimensions.

**Leverage:** how much does this AI use amplify your capability beyond what you could do alone? Low-leverage use makes you faster at things you could already do. High-leverage use enables you to do things that were previously impossible — too slow, too complex, too far outside your expertise.

**Dependency:** how much does this AI use reduce your ability to function without it? Low-dependency use is additive — AI helps, but if it disappeared you could still do the work. High-dependency use is structural — without AI, a significant part of your function degrades.

These two dimensions produce a 2×2 with four quadrants.

**High leverage, low dependency: the sweet spot.** Using AI to explore domains outside your expertise, stress-test assumptions, model scenarios you couldn't construct manually, learn unfamiliar frameworks. You gain capability you wouldn't have had. If AI disappears, you retain what you learned.

**High leverage, high dependency: the trap.** Using AI for tasks central to your professional function that you've stopped being able to do independently. The output quality is high. The risk is that your judgment has been delegated along with the execution.

**Low leverage, low dependency: waste.** Using AI for simple tasks that don't require much intelligence. Not harmful, but not compounding either.

**Low leverage, high dependency: the worst case.** Using AI for routine tasks while building structural reliance on it. Director A is drifting toward this quadrant: her AI use is concentrated in correspondence drafting and report formatting — low-cognitive-demand tasks where the AI provides modest leverage but where the dependency is real.

## Where most professionals actually land

The uncomfortable finding, when you run this audit honestly, is that most professional AI use clusters in the wrong quadrants.

The most commonly reported AI use cases in knowledge work — drafting emails, summarising documents, formatting reports, creating presentation templates — are predominantly low-leverage. They are faster. They are convenient. They produce visible output that managers notice. They are not building the skills, knowledge, or judgment that compound over time.

This is the AI version of a general principle in professional development: the tasks that produce visible short-term output are rarely the ones that build long-term capability. The consultants who spent their evenings reading about industries adjacent to their current engagement were not producing visible output. They were building the pattern recognition that would eventually make them better than their peers at identifying what actually matters in a new engagement.

AI makes the low-leverage, high-visibility option even more seductive because it produces high-quality output with minimal effort. Director A produces excellent ministerial correspondence and well-formatted reports. She looks productive. She is productive. She is also running in place.

## Why convenience bias explains the distribution

The distribution of AI use toward low-leverage tasks is not random. It follows a predictable pattern: the tasks that are most natural to delegate to AI are the ones where you already have a clear idea of what you want and need help producing it. "Draft a letter saying X" is a clear task with a clear output. "Help me develop a view on whether Ireland's corporate tax policy provides a transferable model for our current situation" is harder to delegate — it requires engaging with the output, pushing back, asking follow-up questions, and integrating what you learn into your own thinking.

The second type of use is higher leverage and lower dependency. It is also more effortful. The path of least resistance leads to Director A's pattern.

Recognising this tendency is the first step toward countering it. The shift does not require using AI less — it requires using it differently. The ten hours are not the problem. Where the ten hours go is the problem.

## Practical recalibration

The recalibration exercise: map your last ten AI interactions on the 2×2. Be honest about both axes. For each interaction, ask: did this give me capability I couldn't have had otherwise? Did it reduce my independent capacity to do something I need to be able to do?

Most professionals who complete this exercise find sixty to seventy percent of their AI use in the lower-leverage quadrants. This is useful information.

The follow-up is not to feel bad about the low-leverage use — some of it is genuinely appropriate. It is to identify one or two specific high-leverage uses that you are not currently doing and commit to building them into your weekly practice.

For Director B, the high-leverage uses came from asking: what do I need to understand that I currently don't, and that my official reading wouldn't cover? That question points toward exactly the kind of AI use that builds compounding advantage.

Director A and Director B log the same hours. In five years, their professional positions will not be comparable.

Ten hours of AI use per week is not a strategy.

Where those ten hours go is the strategy.

Most people have never looked.
