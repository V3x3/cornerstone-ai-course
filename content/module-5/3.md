A policy analyst in Sofia receives a two-hundred-page EU regulatory impact assessment. She needs to brief the deputy minister by Thursday. It's Monday. Other things are also due Thursday.

She feeds the document to AI. She gets a clear, well-structured two-page synopsis in four minutes. She reads it carefully. She feels informed.

On Wednesday, at a cross-ministry working group, a colleague from the finance ministry asks about the methodology used in section 4.3 — a particular statistical approach to estimating compliance costs that, as the colleague explains, is contested in the literature and produces systematically different results depending on which version of the model you apply.

She doesn't know what section 4.3 contains. She didn't read section 4.3. She read the summary. The summary mentioned the conclusion the assessment reached on compliance costs. It did not mention that the methodology used to reach that conclusion was disputed, that there was a specific technical choice in section 4.3 that determined the headline number, or that two different EU member states had submitted technical objections to this methodology during the consultation period.

The summary was accurate. It was also incomplete in the specific way that mattered.

She lost three months of credibility in a forty-five-second exchange. The feeling of understanding and the reality of understanding are neurologically indistinguishable — until the moment they diverge in public.

## The illusion AI creates

There is a specific cognitive illusion that AI summaries produce, and it operates below the level of conscious attention.

Reading is experienced as active engagement with a text. When you read, you are processing language, constructing meaning, making inferences. This cognitive process produces something that feels like understanding — a sense of having engaged with and grasped the material.

AI summarisation short-circuits this process in a way that leaves the feeling of understanding intact without the underlying engagement. You read the summary and your brain marks the topic as "known." The neural state that registers as "I have read this document" has been achieved. The knowledge that requires having actually read the document has not been acquired, but the internal register doesn't distinguish between them.

This is not a flaw in your intelligence. It is a structural feature of how comprehension is represented in cognition. The feeling of understanding is produced by fluent processing of language, not by having engaged with the source material directly. AI summaries produce fluent language. Your brain responds accordingly.

The result: you can be confidently wrong about a document you have "read" — in the specific, consequential way that methodological controversies don't summarise well.

## The volume problem

This matters more in government and policy work than almost anywhere else, and acknowledging that creates a tension worth sitting with.

Government policy work genuinely requires summaries. A senior official in a European ministry cannot read every document that crosses her desk — the volume of EU regulatory output, consultation responses, technical annexes, and interinstitutional correspondence is simply too large. Some form of summarisation and prioritisation is not optional. It is a structural necessity.

The question is not whether to use summaries. The question is which documents require genuine deep engagement and which can safely be summarised.

The Sofia analyst's error was not using AI to summarise a two-hundred-page document. Her error was treating a contested, high-stakes document as if it were routine background reading. The compliance cost methodology in section 4.3 was not a technical footnote — it was the load-bearing claim of the entire assessment, and it was disputed. That category of document requires engagement with the original, particularly the contested sections.

The discipline is triage: understanding which documents fall into which category, and applying genuine reading effort where the stakes are high and the complexity is real, while using AI assistance where it is genuinely adequate.

Knowing which three percent of documents require close reading is itself a professional skill. It requires understanding enough about the substantive debates in your domain to recognise when a document is likely to contain contested methodologies, political choices embedded in technical language, or minority views that might later become significant.

## Three tools for testing your understanding

There are practical ways to test whether you have understood a document or have merely been summarised to it.

**The Feynman test.** After reading an AI summary, try to explain the document to someone unfamiliar with it — without looking at the summary. If you cannot explain it clearly, you don't understand it. The gaps in your explanation identify exactly what the summary gave you the feeling of knowing without the substance of knowing.

**The stress test.** Ask AI to challenge the document's methodology and conclusions. Ask it to find the strongest objection a sceptical reader might raise. If the objections surprise you — if you didn't know there were objections, or couldn't have anticipated what they would be — you didn't understand the document. You understood the conclusion the document reached. These are different things.

**The provenance check.** For the three most important claims in the AI summary, identify exactly where in the original document they come from. Not the page number — the actual passage. If you cannot do this, the claim is floating in your mind without grounding. When challenged, it will not hold.

None of these is onerous for genuinely important documents. All of them take a few minutes. All of them would have caught the Sofia analyst before Wednesday.

## The rule, stated simply

If the stakes are high, read the original. If you don't have time to read the original, know what you don't know.

"I read the summary" is not a claim to have understood the document. It is a claim to know what the document said, at the resolution the summary provides. These are different epistemic positions, and conflating them is the error.

The professional who has developed genuine epistemic humility about AI summaries is the one who says, in the cross-ministry meeting: "I've read the summary — I'll need to come back to you on section 4.3 specifically." This is not weakness. This is the most credible possible response.

You've read summaries of things you think you understand. You're wrong about at least three of them.

The question is: which three?
